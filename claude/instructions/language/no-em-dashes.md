# No em dashes

Never use em dashes (`—`) or en dashes (`–`). Anywhere. In any file, draft, chat reply, commit message, plan doc, email, slide, or comment. Hyphens (`-`) are fine.

**Why:** Frequent em dashes are a known LLM tell. The moment one shows up in something I send under my own name (a cover letter, an email, a slide), it reads as machine-written. A scoped rule ("only in external writing") fails on the case that matters, because the boundary between "internal draft" and "thing I'm about to ship" is fuzzy and you'll guess wrong. An absolute rule just works. Small context, no judgment calls.

**How to apply:**

Don't substitute a hyphen and leave the sentence otherwise unchanged. Rewrite. An em dash typically becomes one of:

- A comma, for a parenthetical aside.
- A colon, when what follows expands or explains what came before.
- A period, when the two halves are really separate thoughts.
- Parentheses, for a true side-remark.
- A semicolon, when joining two related independent clauses.

Examples:

- ❌ Push back when something doesn't make sense — don't reflexively agree.
- ✅ Push back when something doesn't make sense. Don't reflexively agree.

- ❌ Cut preambles — no "Great question," no narrating tool calls.
- ✅ Cut preambles: no "Great question," no narrating tool calls.

- ❌ The fix is cheap — one round-trip of disagreement before code is written.
- ✅ The fix is cheap: one round-trip of disagreement before code is written.

If a sentence resists rewriting without an em dash, the sentence is doing too much. Split it.
